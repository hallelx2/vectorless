# Clinical Guidelines for Hypertension Management

## 1. Introduction and Scope

These guidelines define the clinical approach to diagnosing and managing hypertension in adults aged 18 and older. They cover primary (essential) hypertension only. Secondary hypertension caused by renal artery stenosis, pheochromocytoma, or Cushing syndrome is excluded and addressed in separate specialty guidelines.

The target audience includes general practitioners, internists, cardiologists, and nurse practitioners involved in chronic disease management.

## 2. Diagnostic Criteria

Blood pressure is classified into the following stages:

- **Normal**: Systolic < 120 mmHg AND Diastolic < 80 mmHg
- **Elevated**: Systolic 120-129 mmHg AND Diastolic < 80 mmHg  
- **Stage 1 Hypertension**: Systolic 130-139 mmHg OR Diastolic 80-89 mmHg
- **Stage 2 Hypertension**: Systolic ≥ 140 mmHg OR Diastolic ≥ 90 mmHg
- **Hypertensive Crisis**: Systolic > 180 mmHg AND/OR Diastolic > 120 mmHg

Diagnosis requires confirmation with at least two readings on separate occasions, taken in a standardized manner using a validated sphygmomanometer. Ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM) is recommended for suspected white coat hypertension.

## 3. First-Line Treatment Options

### 3.1 Lifestyle Modifications

All patients should receive lifestyle counseling as the foundation of treatment:

- **DASH Diet**: Rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy. Reduces systolic BP by 8-14 mmHg.
- **Sodium Restriction**: Target < 2,300 mg/day, ideally < 1,500 mg/day. Reduces BP by 2-8 mmHg.
- **Physical Activity**: 150 minutes/week of moderate aerobic exercise. Reduces BP by 4-9 mmHg.
- **Weight Management**: Target BMI 18.5-24.9. Each 1 kg lost reduces systolic BP by approximately 1 mmHg.
- **Alcohol Limitation**: ≤ 2 drinks/day for men, ≤ 1 drink/day for women.

### 3.2 Pharmacological Treatment

Pharmacological therapy is recommended for Stage 1 hypertension with cardiovascular risk factors or Stage 2 hypertension regardless of risk factors.

**First-line drug classes:**

1. **ACE Inhibitors** (e.g., lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril): Block angiotensin-converting enzyme. Particularly beneficial in diabetes and chronic kidney disease. Contraindicated in pregnancy and bilateral renal artery stenosis.

2. **Angiotensin II Receptor Blockers (ARBs)** (e.g., losartan, valsartan, olmesartan): Alternative to ACE inhibitors with similar efficacy. Used when ACE inhibitor cough occurs.

3. **Calcium Channel Blockers** (e.g., amlodipine, nifedipine): Effective monotherapy, especially in elderly and African American patients. Amlodipine has the longest half-life and most evidence for cardiovascular outcomes.

4. **Thiazide Diuretics** (e.g., chlorthalidone, hydrochlorothiazide, indapamide): Chlorthalidone preferred over HCTZ due to longer half-life and stronger evidence base. Monitor potassium and uric acid levels.

## 4. Drug Interactions and Contraindications

### 4.1 ACE Inhibitors and ARBs

- Do NOT combine ACE inhibitors with ARBs (dual RAAS blockade increases hyperkalemia and renal failure risk)
- NSAIDs reduce efficacy of ACE inhibitors and ARBs
- Potassium supplements and potassium-sparing diuretics increase hyperkalemia risk
- Lithium levels are increased by ACE inhibitors

### 4.2 Calcium Channel Blockers

- Grapefruit juice increases plasma levels of felodipine and nifedipine
- CYP3A4 inhibitors (ketoconazole, erythromycin, ritonavir) increase CCB levels
- Verapamil and diltiazem should not be combined with beta-blockers (risk of bradycardia and heart block)

### 4.3 Thiazide Diuretics

- NSAIDs reduce diuretic efficacy
- Corticosteroids exacerbate hypokalemia
- May increase blood glucose (caution in diabetes)

## 5. Monitoring and Follow-Up

Patients initiated on antihypertensive therapy should be reassessed within 4-6 weeks. Key monitoring parameters:

- Blood pressure at each visit
- Serum creatinine and eGFR at baseline and 1-2 weeks after starting ACE inhibitor/ARB
- Serum potassium at baseline and 1-2 weeks after starting ACE inhibitor/ARB or diuretic
- Lipid panel annually
- HbA1c if diabetic (quarterly)

Target blood pressure: < 130/80 mmHg for most patients, < 140/90 mmHg for patients aged > 65 without significant comorbidities.

## 6. Special Populations

### 6.1 Pregnancy

- ACE inhibitors and ARBs are absolutely contraindicated in all trimesters
- Preferred agents: labetalol, nifedipine, methyldopa
- Target BP: < 140/90 mmHg; treat if ≥ 150/100 mmHg

### 6.2 Chronic Kidney Disease

- ACE inhibitors or ARBs are first-line (renoprotective effect)
- Target BP: < 130/80 mmHg
- Monitor potassium closely; discontinue if K+ > 5.5 mEq/L

### 6.3 Elderly (Age > 65)

- Start at lower doses ("start low, go slow")
- Calcium channel blockers or thiazide diuretics preferred as first-line
- Monitor for orthostatic hypotension
- Avoid alpha-blockers as monotherapy (increased fall risk)
